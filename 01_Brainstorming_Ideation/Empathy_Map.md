# Empathy Map — Persona: "Ananya, Second-Year CS Student"

## THINK & FEEL
- "Am I the only one who doesn't get this?"
- Worries that asking for help repeatedly makes her look behind.
- Feels a mix of curiosity and dread before opening a new assignment.

## SEE
- Classmates posting confident answers in group chats.
- A wall of dense textbook text with no adaptive support.
- Generic chatbots that don't acknowledge how she's feeling, only the raw question.

## SAY & DO
- Re-reads the same paragraph multiple times without asking anyone.
- Searches "why is recursion so hard" late at night instead of asking a TA.
- Switches tabs / disengages when a topic feels boring rather than pushing through.

## HEAR
- "Just practice more" — well-meaning but unspecific advice from peers.
- Lecture pace assumes everyone is following along equally.
- Occasional encouragement from instructors, but not tied to her specific sticking point.

## PAIN POINTS
- No way to communicate *how* she feels about a topic, only *what* she doesn't understand.
- Generic help doesn't account for her field (CS) or her emotional state (Confused vs Frustrated needs different framing).
- No visibility, for herself or her instructor, into recurring emotional patterns across sessions.

## GAINS (what she wants)
- To type her problem and actual feeling and get a response that meets her where she is.
- To see her own "emotional journey" over a semester, not just grades.
- Reassurance that frustration/confusion is a normal, temporary part of learning.

## Design Implications
These insights directly motivated: (1) capturing both **field** and
**free-text problem description**, (2) detecting emotion *and* mixed
emotion rather than a single label, (3) generating empathetic, field-aware
responses instead of generic tips, and (4) the analytics dashboard that
visualizes emotional trends over a session.
